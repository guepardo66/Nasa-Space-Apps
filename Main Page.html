What is EMIT?


The device on the International Space Station (ISS) called EMIT collects information about the color and composition of the surface of dry, dusty, and windy regions on Earth. These regions are capable of producing mineral dust that can be transported for thousands of miles by the wind. EMIT provides much more detailed information about the composition and color of the surface mineral of Earth's most prolific dust-producing areas than what was previously available. Researchers will use this information to improve climate models that currently rely on assumptions about the color of dust but can calculate its temperature effects in the atmosphere....

Science of Dust


Millions of tons of dust are lifted by the wind from the Earth's driest, sparsely vegetated regions every year and scattered around the Earth. The composition of dust typically shares the same mineral and chemical structure as the rocks and soils in its source areas. Depending on this composition, it exhibits different colors: Dust rich in iron is usually dark red and tends to absorb more energy, while dust rich in clay is pale yellow or white and is more reflective. Mineral dust has various impacts on our planet. It can assist in cloud formation or alter atmospheric chemistry. When dust settles on water or land, it can provide nutrients for ecosystem growth. If it falls on snow or ice, mineral dust can absorb more sunlight and accelerate melting. Suspended in the air, mineral dust can reduce visibility or pose health risks to humans. Understanding how these different effects occur is important, and EMIT plays a crucial role in this understanding
